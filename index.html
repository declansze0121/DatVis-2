From Bean to Cup

Tracing Australia's coffee supply chain — from global origins to Melbourne's café streets

FIT2179 · Data Visualisation 2 · 2026

Australians are passionate about coffee. The nation imports nearly 95 million kilograms of green coffee beans each year from over 50 countries, fuelling a café industry worth billions of dollars. Yet few people know where their daily flat white actually comes from, or how Melbourne — often called the coffee capital of Australia — has grown its café scene over two decades.

This visualisation traces that journey in two parts: the global supply chain that brings raw beans to Australia, and the local café landscape of Melbourne where those beans are roasted, brewed, and served.

The Global Picture

Where do Australia's beans come from? Tracing the supply chain across more than 50 countries.

Where Does Australia's Coffee Come From?

Australia imports virtually all of its coffee — domestic production is negligible. The map below shows every country that exported green (unroasted) coffee beans to Australia in 2023, coloured by trade value. Latin America dominates, but significant volumes also arrive from Africa and Asia.

Data source: UN Comtrade via World Bank WITS, 2023. HS Code 090111 (Coffee, not roasted or decaffeinated). Log colour scale used to reveal smaller origins.

Brazil is by far Australia's largest coffee supplier, accounting for over $112 million in green bean imports — roughly a quarter of the total. Colombia, Honduras, Papua New Guinea, and Vietnam round out the top five. Together, these five countries supply about 64% of Australia's coffee by value.

Just five countries account for nearly two-thirds of everything Australia imports — yet the other 46 origins tell a richer story of diversity and specialty.

Part Two

Price, Volume & Quality

Not all beans are equal. Exploring the wide spectrum from bulk commodity coffee to rare specialty lots.

Not All Beans Are Equal

The scatter plot below reveals an important nuance in Australia's coffee trade: price per kilogram varies dramatically between origins. Some countries sell premium specialty beans at high prices in small quantities, while others supply commodity-grade coffee in bulk at lower per-kilogram costs.

Filter by region using the dropdown  ·  Hover a bubble to spotlight it and see details

Each bubble represents one origin country. Bubble size encodes total import value. Note the logarithmic x-axis — the volume gap between top and bottom origins spans several orders of magnitude.

Vietnam, for example, supplies large volumes at a relatively low price per kilogram (~$2.58/kg), while Jamaican beans command over $61/kg — reflecting Jamaica Blue Mountain's reputation as one of the world's most prized coffees. This price spectrum reflects the difference between commodity coffee (used for instant and blends) and specialty coffee (single-origin, craft roasts).

From $2.58/kg to over $61/kg — Australia's coffee imports span a 24× price range, from commodity bulk to rare single-estate lots.

How Do the Origins Score on Quality?

By combining import data with the Coffee Quality Institute's cupping database, we can compare the flavour profiles of Australia's top suppliers. Each bean is scored on six attributes: aroma, flavour, aftertaste, acidity, body, and balance.

Data source: Coffee Quality Institute (CQI) via GitHub. Averages computed from arabica samples with 5+ reviews per country. Only countries that also appear in Australia's import data are shown.

The chart below directly tests whether Australia pays more for higher-quality coffee. Each dot represents a country positioned by its CQI quality score (x-axis) and how much Australia spends importing from it (y-axis). The relationship is not straightforward — Colombia and Ethiopia score high and attract high import spend, but Brazil dominates by sheer volume despite moderate quality scores.

This chart combines two independent datasets — UN Comtrade trade data and CQI cupping scores — to explore whether quality predicts import spending. Bubble size encodes import quantity.

The City Where Coffee Comes to Life

Melbourne is widely regarded as Australia's coffee capital, with one of the densest café cultures in the world. Using the City of Melbourne's Census of Land Use and Employment (CLUE) data, we can map every café and restaurant across the CBD and surrounding suburbs.

Map shows a representative sample of 600 establishments from 2024. Point colour encodes the suburb area. Hover for details. Data: City of Melbourne CLUE, industry code 4511 (Cafés and Restaurants).

The growth of Melbourne's café scene over two decades tells a remarkable story. From around 679 establishments in 2002 to over 3,400 in 2024, the number of cafés and restaurants has increased fivefold — with only a brief dip during the COVID-19 pandemic.

Melbourne's café count grew by 5× in two decades — one new venue for roughly every two days, every year, for 22 years.

The Melbourne CBD unsurprisingly dominates, but suburbs like Southbank, Docklands, and Carlton have grown significantly. The heatmap below shows how each area's café density has evolved year by year.

Finally, a look at seating capacity reveals how different suburbs balance indoor and outdoor dining. The CBD has the highest total seat count, but suburbs like Southbank and Carlton offer a proportionally higher share of outdoor seating — reflecting their waterfront and parkside settings.

From Bean to Cup — The Full Picture

Australia's coffee story is one of quiet complexity. On the surface it is simply a morning ritual — a flat white, a long black, a table at a sunny Melbourne laneway. But behind each cup lies a global supply chain spanning more than 50 countries, $439 million in annual trade, and decades of shifting taste.

The data tells three interconnected stories. First, geography shapes flavour: the lion's share of Australia's beans come from Latin America, but Asia-Pacific origins like Papua New Guinea and Vietnam play a significant and growing role. Second, price is not a simple proxy for quality: Brazil dominates by sheer volume at mid-range prices, while Ethiopia and Colombia command both high scores and high spend — and Jamaica Blue Mountain sits in a category of its own.

Third, and perhaps most visibly, Melbourne's café culture is a genuine phenomenon. Five times more venues today than in 2002. A brief but sharp COVID dip that the industry quickly reversed. A suburban café scene spreading beyond the CBD into Carlton, Southbank, and Docklands. The numbers confirm what most Australians already sense: coffee is not just a beverage here — it is a way of life.
